œá2 Test of Goodness of Fit

The \(\chi^2\) (\(\chi\) reads ‚Äúki‚Äù) test of goodness of fit refers to a test used to check whether some values occur at different rates than what you would expect. The simplest possible example is checking if a coin is fair (i.e., heads and tails come up 50% of the times):

I flipped my coin 200 times and I got 110 heads and 90 tails:

flips <- c(110, 90)

chisq.test(flips)

    Chi-squared test for given probabilities

data:  flips
X-squared = 2, df = 1, p-value = 0.1573

The null and alternative hypotheses are:

  • \(H_0\): The coin is fair
  • \(H_1\): The coin is not fair

The chisq.test() function takes in either a vector or table of counts and runs a \(\chi^2\) test.


Thus, at \(\alpha = .05\), there wasn‚Äôt enough evidence to reject the \(H_0\), \(\chi^2(1) = 2\), \(p = .16\).

œá2 Test by Hand

Sometimes I like to run these analyses ‚Äúby hand‚Äù to check that I am doing the right thing. In the lecture you have seen that the formula to calculate the \(\chi^2\) statistic is given by:

\[\chi^2 = \sum_i \frac{(O_i - E_i)^2}{E_i}\] Where \(O_i\) is the observed proportion of a category and \(E_i\) is the expected proportion of that category. Since we have 200 flips, if the coin is fair, we expect both tails and head to happen 100 times each (\(E_i = 100\) in both cases), so:

# this is the formula above written in R (scary math can be done pretty quickly in R)
sum(((flips - 100)^2)/100)
[1] 2
# this gives the p-value (DFs in this case is always going to be the number of categories - 1)
pchisq(2, df = 1, lower.tail = FALSE)
[1] 0.1572992

Of course you would always use the chisq.test() function in practice, but I want to briefly touch on the intuition behind the \(\chi^2\) formula.

Intuition Behind the œá2 Statistic

Unfortunately many statistics formulas seem to come out of nowhere ü§∑ \(\chi^2 = \sum_i \frac{(O_i - E_i)^2}{E_i}\) is no exception. However, you can often gain a good sense of what the formula is doing by breaking down its components:

  • \(\sum_i\): This sign says ‚Äúsum all the i things‚Äù. If you recall, this sign was also in the mean and the SD formula. In 99% of the cases, the \(\sum\) sign is a tell that the formula is trying to summarize some information.
  • \((O_i - E_i)^2\): The numerator is usually where the important stuff happens. Here, the numerator calculates how different the observed count is from what we expected. The \(^2\) makes everything positive so that differences don‚Äôt cancel out when you ‚Äúsum all the i things‚Äù (the SD formula does the same!).
  • \(E_i\): In most formulas the denominator makes sure the numerator is adjusted before been summed. In the case of the \(\chi^2\), it adjusts for how often you expect to see each value.

So, the \(\chi^2\) formula summarizes how different your observed data is compared to what you would expect to see. The more unexpected your observed frequencies, the larger the \(\chi^2\) statistic will be.

œá2 Test of Independence

The \(\chi^2\) test of independence is actually the same exact test as before; however, we call it test of independence when we use use it to analyze contingency tables. For example let‚Äôs create a table with delays by two airplane companies, Delta and American Airlines:

I create a new data.frame that only includes Delta and AA:

AA_vs_DL <- flights %>%
              filter(Airline %in% c("AA", "DL"))

And then I create a table:

tabl <- table(AA_vs_DL$Airline, AA_vs_DL$Delay)
tabl
    
     On Time Delay
  AA   27920 17736
  DL   33488 27452

The null and alternative hypotheses are:

  • \(H_0\): Airlines and delays are independent
  • \(H_1\): Airlines and delays are not independent 
chisq.test(tabl)

    Pearson's Chi-squared test with Yates' continuity correction

data:  tabl
X-squared = 410.66, df = 1, p-value < 2.2e-16

We reject \(H_0\), \(\chi^2 (1) = 410.66\), \(p < .001\), meaning that there is some association between airline and delay.

Easily Find Expected Frequencies

Even in the case of a table the formula to calculate the \(\chi^2\) statistic is still \(\sum_i \frac{(O_i - E_i)^2}{E_i}\). Although it does not change the overall logic, the expected cell frequencies, \(E_i\), is calculated in a slightly different way: \(E_i =\frac{R_{tot} \times C_{\mathrm{tot}}}{Total}\), where

  • \(R_{tot}\): total number of observations in row.
  • \(C_{tot}\): total number of observations in column.
  • \(Total\): Is the sum of all values in the table (the sample size).

You do not have to do this by hand. You can do the following:

chi_test <- chisq.test(tabl)
chi_test$expected
    
      On Time    Delay
  AA 26301.58 19354.42
  DL 35106.42 25833.58

This works because the chisq.test() function actually saves a lot of information when you save it as an object.

names(chi_test)
[1] "statistic" "parameter" "p.value"   "method"    "data.name" "observed" 
[7] "expected"  "residuals" "stdres"

The same is true for many of the functions that we will see later in teh course.

‚ÄúSome Association‚Äù? What does that Mean?

You may find the some association part from two slides ago a bit vague‚Ä¶and you‚Äôd be right! All the \(\chi^2\) test we just ran tells us is that there is some association somewhere in out table. In our case it is easy to figure out which airline has a higher proportion of delays, but it becomes much harder when the number of rows and columns increases üßê

Once again the \(\chi^2\) formula summarize how different \(O_i\)s are from \(E_i\)s, how different the data is from what we would expect if \(H_0\) is true. The idea is that, based on probability theory:

the only way that we observe something that is on average far from our expected frequencies is if there is some association between the rows and the columns

Thus, it would be really unlikely to see a table of observed frequencies that is different that the expected if \(H_0\) is true and that is no association. Notice that this is \(H_1\) from two slides ago and is the only thing that you are testing when running a \(\chi^2\) test ü§î

Effect Sizes For œá2 Tests

Ok, so, we know that there is some association between airlines and delays (\(\chi^2 (1) = 410.66\), \(p < .001\)), but we really do not know how practically meaningful the strength of the association is (many statistically significant results are often practically meaningless ü´†). This is where effect sizes come into the picture. Effect sizes are meant to be measures of practical significance, and they often have little relation with statistical significance.
There are two popular effect sizes for \(\chi^2\) tests
\(W\) Coefficient

\(W = \sqrt{\frac{\chi^2}{Total}}\)

# sum(tabl) sums all the values in the table, thus giving the total
sqrt(410.66/sum(tabl))
[1] 0.06206843
Cramer‚Äôs \(V\)

\(V = \sqrt{\frac{\chi^2}{Total \times (k - 1)}}\), where \(k\) is either the row or column with the smallest number of categories.

# When both columns and rows have 2 categories, W and V are equivalent
sqrt(410.66/(sum(tabl)*(2 - 1)))
[1] 0.06206843

Interpreting Effect Sizes

We got a \(W = .06\). How big (or small) is that effect size? The correct answer is that it depends. The determination usually involves some cost-benefit trade off which depends on the field that you are in.

Now, everyone will reference effect sizes ‚Äúguidelines‚Äù from Cohen (1988), who went through many popular effect sizes metrics and suggested some thresholds for what is a small, medium, and large effect size.

According to Cohen‚Äôs guidelines for \(W\) :

  • \(W = .1\) ‚Üí small
  • \(W = .3\) ‚Üí medium
  • \(W = .5\) ‚Üí large

So, our effect size of \(W = .06\) is small, meaning that the degree of association between airlines and delay is small (i.e., although one airline has more delays, it‚Äôs not by that much).

‚ÄúGuidelines‚Äù? The Unfortunate Truth

The main argument for providing guidelines is that it helps applied researchers to make decisions. The idea is reasonable, but in my experience this sometimes turns into ‚ÄúI don‚Äôt really understand it, so tell me what to do based on these numbers in my output‚Äù. The most obvious case is \(p < .05\), which is probably the main cause of the replication crisis. Unfortunately, we teach to follow these ‚Äúguidelines‚Äù, and this course will be no different (I am not happy about it üòî). When doing statistics in the real world, always stop and ask yourself why you are doing something or making a certain decision. If your answer is ‚Äúbecause someone else said so‚Äù, then you still do not know the why, which is the most important thing.

Likelihood Ratio Test

You may come across the likelihood ratio test, which in the case of a contingency table ends up being quite similar to the \(\chi^2\) test:

\[\chi^2 = 2 \sum_i[O_i \times \ln(\frac{O_i}{E_i}) ]\]

Where \(\ln()\) is the natural logarithm. In R this scary equation is quite simple to compute:

# NOTE: It may be worth spending some time understanding why this line of code works 
like_chi <- 2*sum(tabl*log(tabl/chi_test$expected))
[1] 411.9941

We get a really similar \(\chi^2\) value and therefore a really similar p-value:

# DF are (Nrows - 1)*(Ncols - 1) = (2 - 1)*(2 - 1) = 1
pchisq(like_chi, df = 1, lower.tail = FALSE) 
[1] 1.349157e-91

The classical \(\chi^2\) test generally works best for contingency tables. However, the likelihood ratio test is more general and will come up again because it can be used to compare different statistical models.

Small Expected Frequencies

Some smart math people have worked out that if any of the expected frequencies are too small (\(E_i \leq 5\), not the case of the example), the the type I error rate (more on this in Lab 4) of the \(\chi^2\) test is not accurate. In this case you should apply some adjustments to your \(\chi^2\) statistic. Both of these are fine:

Yates Correction

The chisq.test() function applies this correction by default. You can see it in the output:

chisq.test(tabl)

    Pearson's Chi-squared test with Yates' continuity correction

data:  tabl
X-squared = 410.66, df = 1, p-value < 2.2e-16

This is good because there is no ‚Äúdisadvantage‚Äù to applying this correction, so it may as well be the default.

E. Pearson Adjustment

\(\chi^2_{adj} = \chi^2 \times \frac{N}{N -1}\), where \(N\) is the sample size.

# get unadjusted chi-square (it will be very similar to the adjusted one because our expected proportions are really large)
chi_unadj <- chisq.test(tabl, correct = FALSE)$statistic
X-squared 
 410.9175 
chi_unadj*(sum(tabl)/(sum(tabl) - 1))
X-squared 
 410.9214

The adjustment makes almost no difference because of the really large \(N\).

Fisher‚Äôs Exact Test

When you have small expected frequencies, Fisher‚Äôs exact test is probably a better choice than a \(\chi^2\) test. The why it is a better choice is not straightforward to explain (but let me know if you are curious).

Fisher‚Äôs exact test works by checking whether the odds ratio (see next slides) of the table is significantly different from 1.

fisher.test(tabl)

    Fisher's Exact Test for Count Data

data:  tabl
p-value < 2.2e-16
alternative hypothesis: true odds ratio is not equal to 1
95 percent confidence interval:
 1.258956 1.322845
sample estimates:
odds ratio 
  1.290446

The null and alternative hypotheses are:

  • \(H_0\): the odds ratio is 1
  • \(H_1\): the odds ratio is not 1

The odds ratio is significantly different from 1, odds = 1.29, \(p < .001\), 95% CI[1.26, 1.32]

Fun Fact

Fisher‚Äôs exact test is based on the hypergeometric distribution. The fun fact is that the hypergeometric distribution is the probability distribution that is used to determine the probability of drawing certain hands in card games. Incidentally, the hypergeometric distribution is used a lot in Texas Hold‚Äôem Poker, and any other competitive card game really.

Odds and Probability

Odds and probabilities are two different ways of describing the likelihood of an event occurring. Let‚Äôs say that team A and team B are playing a Basketball game and we think know that team A wind 80% of the times. Let‚Äôs define \(\pi = .8\) as the probability of team A winning. Then, the the odds of team A winning are:

\[\mathrm{odds} = \frac{\pi}{1 - \pi} = \frac{.8}{1 - .8} = 4 \]

This is what you would normally hear as ‚Äúteam A has 4 to 1 odds of beating team B‚Äù. It is important to note that given any odds, we can recover the probability \(\pi\):

\[\pi = \frac{\mathrm{odds}}{\mathrm{odds} + 1} = \frac{4}{4 + 1} = .8\]

Odds: Number of successes compared to number of failures.

Probability: Number of successes compared to total number of trials.

Odds Ratios

In the case of our airplane example, we can look at the odds of both Delta and AA having a delayed flight:

    
     On Time Delay
  AA   27920 17736
  DL   33488 27452

The ‚Äúsuccess‚Äù in this case is the flight being delayed, so it goes on top in the numerator.

# odds for AA 
tabl[1,2]/tabl[1,1]
[1] 0.6352436
# odds for Delta
tabl[2,2]/tabl[2,1]
[1] 0.8197563

Note: if you wanted the odds of flights being on time, you would flip the fractions above.

We see that Delta is more likely to have delays as it‚Äôs odds are higher than AA. But by how much? We can calculate an odds ratio:

\[\mathrm{odds_{ratio}} = \frac{\mathrm{odds_{DL}}}{\mathrm{odds_{AA}}} = \frac{.82}{.63} = 1.3\]

Interpretation: The odds of Delta flights being delayed are 1.3 larger than the odds of AA flights being delayed.

So, an odds ratio is quite literally the ratio of two odds üòÄ

Relative Risk

In epidemiology language, Risk is the exact same as probability. We can get the risk (probability) of either company‚Äôs flights being delayed:

    
     On Time Delay
  AA   27920 17736
  DL   33488 27452

Because risk is probability, now we use the sum of the row in the denominator. Delays are still treated as ‚Äúsuccesses‚Äù:

# risk for AA 
tabl[1,2]/sum(tabl[1,])
[1] 0.3884703
# risk for Delta
tabl[2,2]/sum(tabl[2,])
[1] 0.4504759

How much more likely are Delta flights to be delayed? The relative risk of a Delta flights being delayed compared to AA flights answers this question:

\[\mathrm{risk_{rel}} = \frac{\mathrm{risk_{DL}}}{\mathrm{risk_{AA}}} = \frac{.45}{.39} = 1.15\]

Interpretation: Delta flights are 1.15 more likely to be delayed compared to AA flights.

So odds and risk (probability) have different interpretations. Be careful when you use them or read about them.

e-N?

In R output you will often see some e-N. On the left we see e-91 in the p-values. This is computer notation to represent small numbers. So, 1.134e-3 means 0.001134. Then, e-91 means zero with 90 zeros after the decimal point (pretty small number). This is good because you do not want to see 90 zeros in your output.
